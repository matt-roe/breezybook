The history of the Sheep Ranch Inn


The land that this building is on was a sheep ranch at one time; legend has it that the old little brick shed across the highway at Shultz Auto Body was the home of the sheep rancher.

Our original building was built in the 70’s by Tony (Pauly) Gulli. All the locals refer to it as the old sheep ranch, hence the name!

Thomas & Mary Font purchased the property in 1978 and ran the Sheep Ranch Inn & Supper Club until 1993 when they retired.



In 1993 Alan and Rosie Howe purchased the property and they ran the Sheep Ranch Inn restaurant & bar out of this building. They added the Creekside Bed & Breakfast in the downstairs area of their home next door, a RV park on the other side of the restaurant, and also a 3D archery range back beyond the pond. Many people have wonderful memories here, wether it be where they went on dates during their high school years, family gatherings and celebrations, shooting the 3D archery range, or the karaoke nights in the bar! This studio unit was Rosie’s office.

Rosie Howe passed away in 2005 and subsequently the Bed & Breakfast and home next door, RV park area, pond and land beyond were sold. Other than the home next door, the surrounding land including the bridge & pond is now tribal land. Alan closed the restaurant and took residence in this building, adding walls to divide it into three different living areas. Alan passed away in 2016 and the building sat mostly unoccupied until January 2018.

Matthew Roe purchased the building in January of 2018. He and his family spent the entire year renovating and making improvements before opening the Old Sheep Ranch Inn early the next year.




The history of the Flathead Reservation


The Flathead Indian Reservation is home to the Bitterroot Salish, Kootenai, and Pend d’Oreilles Tribes, together known as the Confederated Salish and Kootenae Tribes of the Flathead Nation. As European-American settlers enter the area, the people came into conflict.

In 1855 the United States government made the Treaty of Hellgate, which set aside reservation sole for the Flathead. Although the tribe opposed the European style allotments and farming, the US Congress passed the 1904 Flathead Allotment Pact. After allotment of land to individual households of members on the tribal rolls, the government declared the rest “surplus” and opened the reservation to homesteading by non-natives. It’s passage caused understandable resentment by the Flathead inhabitants, and the allotment of land remains a very sensitive issue. Thousands of acres on the reservation were reserved for town sites, schools, and the National Bison Range. Part of the Mission Mountain Range is on the reservation. All but the northern tip of Flathead Lake is part of the reservation. The total population living on the reservation is approximately 30,000, with only 1/3 of that population being Native Americans.
